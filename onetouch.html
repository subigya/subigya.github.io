Case Study

One Touch Systems – Product Concept / UX Design / FinTech Innovation

Background

In January 2007, Apple unveiled the first iPhone — a product that fundamentally changed how we interacted with technology. By June, it was available for purchase. Using it for the first time felt like witnessing the future. It was a paradigm shift in design, usability, and what technology could feel like.

That fall, inspired by the iPhone, I developed a concept for my product design class — a device focused solely on financial interactions.

Dimensions based on the original iPhone

Concept

The Device: “One Touch Systems”

The idea was to create a dedicated “wallet” device built for banking and payments. Unlike a phone, this product was singularly focused on secure financial management.

Key Features (2007 Concept)

  • Dedicated SIM card for mobile internet (GPRS at the time)
  • Fingerprint reader for secure ID verification
  • RFID read/write capabilities for contactless payments
  • Local data encryption for secure financial storage

The device’s primary purpose was to enable users to manage their money securely on the go, without relying on a computer or visiting a bank.

3d Modeling in Photoshop

Core Functions

The Flash-based interface (developed in 2007) included five main modules:

  1. Wallet – Select and manage multiple debit/credit cards; set default payment methods.
  2. Records – View transaction history by account or card.
  3. Statements – Access official statements or downloadable summaries.
  4. Transfer – Move money between accounts or make payments.
  5. Setup – Activate, remove, or modify accounts and ownership details.

Much of the original prototype was built in Flash, and SketchUp for 3D rendering of the device and Adobe After Effects for demo video (which I have unfortunately lost). My focus was on learning new tools and visualizing future interactions with digital finance.

Reflection

Looking back, One Touch Systems was ahead of its time conceptually, but limited by available technology and my early UX understanding.

Years later, Apple introduced Touch ID, Apple Pay, and NFC-based Wallet, all of which closely mirrored parts of my 2007 concept — but realized through software rather than dedicated hardware.

At the time, I envisioned the experience as device-based, because I didn’t think such functionality could exist purely in software. Nonetheless, the idea caught some early attention — I even received outreach from an Apple recruiter. Though it didn’t lead to an opportunity, it validated the forward-thinking nature of the project.

Recruiter email from Nov 16, 2007

The Present (2025)

Today, the landscape has completely evolved. Software has replaced hardware as the driving force of financial innovation. Smartphones now include biometric authentication, NFC, and high-speed connectivity — eliminating the need for specialized hardware.

Open Banking standards and APIs (e.g., Plaid, Yodlee) have enabled secure connections between apps and financial institutions. Modern fintech products like Monarch Money, Empower Personal Dashboard, Rocket Money, and Quicken Simplifi now provide comprehensive financial visibility and management across multiple platforms.

Peer-to-peer payment systems such as Zelle, Cash App, and Venmo have also made money transfer seamless and secure.

The Future of FinTech

The next phase of FinTech will likely merge AI-driven personalization, unified account visibility, and contextual automation — helping users not only manage money, but make smarter, more predictive financial decisions.

The original vision of One Touch Systems — a single, secure interface for all financial interactions — is closer than ever, just realized through software ecosystems rather than standalone devices.

Key Learnings & Growth as a Designer

This project was a foundational moment in my growth as a UX and product designer.

  • Systems Thinking: It taught me to look beyond visual design and think in ecosystems — hardware, software, and user context working together.
  • Empathy & Focus: I learned that good design starts with a clear user purpose, not just technology novelty.
  • Adaptability: Working with emerging tools (SketchUp, Flash, ActionScript) reinforced the importance of learning fast to bring ideas to life.
  • Vision & Validation: Seeing similar ideas realized years later validated that forward-thinking matters — but timing, execution, and user context matter more.

That experience still shapes how I approach design today — balancing bold vision with practical UX strategy, and always anchoring innovation in real human value.
