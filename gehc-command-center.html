Case Study

GE Healthcare Command Center

Over 11 years, I’ve shaped the GE Healthcare Command Center, an AI-powered platform transforming hospital operations with real-time patient flow insights. As UX and UI Designer and Developer, I co-designed the Command Center platform.

Project Scope: Full platform UX and UI design and frontend development

My Role: UX and UI design, prototyping, and development (non-js based).

Team: 2-person design team (core), upto 4 designers, collaborating with developers, engineers, product owners, and stakeholders

source: d3.harvard.edu

The Challenge: Overcoming High-Stakes Hurdles

Hospitals struggle with real-time patient flow, bed capacity, and resource management. Leadership teams need a unified, actionable view of critical data without being overwhelmed. Hospital admins and clinicians—needed intuitive tools amid resource strain.

As part of a 2-person core design team, I was responsible for co-designing the entire Command Center platform — from the foundational design system to the individual modules. This included dashboards, main tile modules and companion configuration modules, notifications & alerts, data visualizations, interfaces for both desktop/tablet, mobile(some) and large-scale wall displays and an icon library to complement medical and system alerts.

The platform comprises of more than 39 main tiles (modules) plus additional configuration and specialized modules, making this a large-scale, end-to-end design effort. (Digital Twin component not in my scope.)

source: healthtechmagazine.net

Process

  • Started with ideas → sketches → wireframes → prototypes.
  • Iterated with engineers, product owners, and stakeholders until validated.
  • Conducted an on-site visit to Humber River Hospital (Toronto) after initial development to observe workflows and interview staff — insights confirmed design choices and highlighted areas for improvement.
source: bizjournals.com

Key Solutions and Design Decisions

  • Tile System: 39+ tiles modules used visualizations, iconography, whitespace and hierarchial typography for clarity.
  • Criticality Encoding: Icons with badges and color coded context to drive attention, shining at trade shows.
  • Typography & Hierarchy: With most tiles being content-heavy, emphasis on clear typography, visual hierarchy, and white space to reduce cognitive load.
  • Clarity Over Complexity: Simplified the presentation of massive real-time datasets, ensuring information remained actionable instead of overwhelming.
  • Context-Aware Views: Responsive layouts balanced dense data for desktop and physical use.
  • Iconography: Built a local dynamic library of more than 700+ icons.
source: gehealthcare.com

We iterated through three main phases:

  • Phase 1: First launched in John Hopkins Medicine as Judy Reitz Capacity Command Center in 2016 (AngularJS 1.x)
  • Phase 2: Expanded the command center with more modules, core tile concepts, Adoption by more hospitals throughout the world (Angular)
  • Phase 3: More complex concepts and modules, no more limited to physical space first approach, wider worldwide adoption (Angular latest)

Collaborating with engineers and stakeholders, we refined designs to ensure performance and compliance.

source: bizjournals.com

Challenges

  • Data complexity: Simplifying vast, real-time datasets into actionable insights.
  • Scale & Complexity: 39+ tiles, including configurable views, without overwhelming users.
  • Performance: Optimizing browser-based real-time rendering
  • Regulatory constraints: Designing within strict healthcare compliance standards.
  • Dual Contexts: Seamless UI for desktop and physical command centers.
  • Containment: Tiles couldn’t rely on cloud assets (e.g., icons, libraries) outside hospital netwwork or infrastructure due to architecture restrictions.
source: koreaherald.com

Impact

Command Center has been deployed globally, helping hospitals:

  • Reduce patient length of stay
  • Improve operational efficiency
  • Empower leadership to make faster, data-driven decisions
source: hrhfoundation.ca

Lessons Learned:

Over 11 years on this project, I learned that design principles endure even as tools evolve. This sharpened my skill to simplify enterprise complexity. If I could go back, I’d push for more direct, ongoing end-user research rather than relying mainly on product owner input.
